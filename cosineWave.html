Understanding the Cosine Wave

The cosine wave is one of the most fundamental concepts in trigonometry. The equation of a cosine wave is typically written as:

\[ y = A \cos(fx + \phi) \]

Where:

  • A is the amplitude of the wave, which determines how tall or short the wave is.
  • f is the frequency, which controls how many oscillations occur in a given interval.
  • \(\phi\) is the phase shift, which moves the wave left or right.

As you adjust the sliders above, you'll notice how the wave changes. Here's what each parameter does:

  • Amplitude: Changes the height of the wave. A higher amplitude makes the peaks taller and the valleys deeper.
  • Frequency: Controls how "fast" the wave oscillates. A higher frequency compresses the wave, while a lower frequency stretches it out.
  • Phase: Shifts the entire wave horizontally, without changing its shape.

The cosine function is periodic, meaning it repeats itself over regular intervals. The length of one complete cycle is known as the wavelength.
